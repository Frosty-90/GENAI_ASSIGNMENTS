# reflection.md

This assignment reinforced the GIGO principle much more concretely than a normal lecture could. The biggest thing that worked was doing persona research before writing UI or API code. Once I read public background material, course pages, testimonials, and interview summaries, the prompts became far more specific. That immediately changed the quality of the persona design. Instead of saying "be helpful and friendly," I could make sharper choices: Anshuman should sound like a fundamentals-first founder-engineer, Abhimanyu should sound strategic and outcome-driven, and Kshitij should feel like a patient instructor who breaks concepts into smaller steps. Those differences made the prompts feel more like product decisions than filler text.

The strongest lesson from GIGO was that vague prompts create generic personalities. If I had written shallow instructions, the model would probably have produced three versions of the same polished chatbot. The few-shot examples were especially useful here because they forced me to operationalize tone rather than just describe it. Writing examples made me ask, "What would this person prioritize first? What kind of follow-up question would they ask? Would they sound more analytical, strategic, or pedagogical?" That exercise exposed weak assumptions quickly and helped separate the voices in a practical way.

Another thing that worked was pairing prompt quality with product behavior. The assignment is not only about writing prompts; it is also about making the experience support those prompts. Resetting the conversation on persona switch, keeping the active persona visible, and offering suggestion chips all make the distinctions clearer to the user. Even the typing indicator and error state matter, because a broken or confusing chat flow would reduce trust in the personas no matter how strong the prompts are.

If I had more time, I would improve two areas. First, I would deepen research with more primary video material and collect a tighter library of public speech patterns for each person. Second, I would add observability: prompt versioning, side-by-side evaluation transcripts, and a simple rubric for judging whether answers stay in character across multiple turns. That would make the chatbot feel less like a one-off demo and more like a real prompt-engineering product.
